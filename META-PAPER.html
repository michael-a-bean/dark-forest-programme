AI as Research Infrastructure

A Case Study in Dissertation-Scale Computational Science Using GEN/PAI/Claude Code

Author
Affiliation

Michael Bean

Independent Researcher

Published

March 25, 2026

Abstract

This paper documents the research process behind a doctoral dissertation comprising five papers and one methodological supplement in computational artificial life, produced over twelve days using GEN, a personal AI assistant built on PAI (Personal AI Infrastructure) running on Claude Code with Claude Opus 4.6. The process involved autonomous experiment design and distributed cluster execution (approximately 15,000 simulation jobs producing 4.6 million session-level observations), simulated dissertation committee meetings with persistent AI personas, AI-conducted peer review, comprehensive parallel literature search across five API backends, a multi-phase academic integrity audit, and a mock doctoral defence with external examiners. We describe the technical architecture, the roles played by AI agents at each stage, failure modes encountered, and findings that converged with prior literature the system had not consulted. The case raises questions about the nature of discovery when the researcher, committee, reviewers, and auditors operate as AI systems under the direction of a single human principal.

Keywords

AI-assisted research, personal AI infrastructure, computational artificial life, research methodology, academic process

1 Introduction

None of this was planned.

On March 13, 2026, Michael Bean opened a conversation with GEN — his personal AI assistant — about Deleuze’s concept of differenciation and whether goalless dynamics could produce qualitatively different kinds from identical starting conditions. There was no research agenda, no proposal, no intention to write a paper. It was a late-night question driven by curiosity: could a Hebbian network, left entirely to its own devices, produce something that Deleuze would recognise as genuine difference?

The conversation led to code. The code produced results that were interesting enough to write up. Writing up raised questions that required more experiments. The experiments revealed a confound that changed the interpretation of everything that came before. At each step, the decision to continue was made in the moment, not according to a plan. The “dissertation” framing emerged organically when the accumulated work reached the scale where it needed a structure — and the simulated committee, peer review, and defence emerged as tools for pressure-testing that structure, not as ends in themselves.

Michael’s background informed the direction at every turn. His interest in Deleuze and Simondon shaped the philosophical framework. His frustration with metrics that measure the wrong thing led to the CCD invalidation and the adoption of response rank. His instinct that “the pruning shouldn’t matter” led to the experiment that showed it mattered enormously. The AI system executed and elaborated, but the questions — and the dissatisfaction with easy answers — came from the human.

Twelve days later, the conversation had produced approximately 15,000 simulation jobs on a 20$$20-node substrate, 4.6 million session-level observations, five completed papers, a dissertation assembly, and a simulated defence. This document exists because the process itself turned out to be as interesting as the findings.

What this document is not. It is not a formal methods paper. It is not a proposal for how AI-assisted research should be conducted. It is a record of how one human and one AI system followed a question where it led, using the tools available, and what they found along the way — both about Hebbian self-organisation and about the process of AI-assisted inquiry.

What was real and what was simulated. The computational experiments executed on a physical Ray cluster and produced real data. The scientific findings are genuine results of those computations. The academic process surrounding them — committee meetings, peer review, editorial decisions, and the doctoral defence — was entirely simulated using AI agents within a single conversation session. No papers were submitted to any journal. No committee held institutional authority. No defence had academic standing. The simulated processes served as quality-control mechanisms and stress tests, not as claims of academic legitimacy.

2 The Technical Stack

2.1 GEN and PAI

GEN is the human principal’s personal AI assistant, built on the Personal AI Infrastructure (PAI) framework created by Daniel Miessler (Miessler, 2024). PAI extends Claude Code (Anthropic, 2025) with capabilities that proved essential for sustained research:

Agent orchestration. PAI enables spawning specialised sub-agents — Architect, Engineer, Explore, and multiple researcher types (Codex, Gemini, Grok, Perplexity, Claude) — for parallel task execution. Each agent operates with its own context but inherits the underlying model. This architecture enabled the parallel committee meetings, fourteen-agent literature reviews, and multi-phase audits documented below.

Persistent memory. A file-based memory system preserves project context, learning signals, and work items across conversation sessions. Two research documents that survived from the programme’s first day — “Capacities, Intent, and Affordances” (a synthesis of Gibson and DeLanda) and “Qualitative Differentiation from Homogeneous Substrates” (a reading of Deleuze’s Difference and Repetition) — demonstrate that philosophical research preceded and shaped the experimental design.

Skill routing. Modular capabilities invoked by slash commands provide structured workflows. The Research skill, for example, routes to appropriate agent configurations (quick, standard, extensive, or deep investigation) based on the scope of the query.

The Algorithm. A structured multi-phase reasoning protocol for complex tasks that enforces systematic observation, planning, and execution rather than immediate response.

2.2 Claude Code and Claude Opus 4.6

Claude Code (Anthropic, 2025) provides the runtime: direct filesystem access, bash execution, git operations, and multi-tool orchestration within a persistent working directory. The underlying model, Claude Opus 4.6, offers a one-million-token context window — a constraint that proved load-bearing. This session accumulated context across experiment monitoring, six committee meetings, five peer review rounds, a fourteen-agent literature synthesis, and paper revision, all within a single continuous conversation.

2.3 The Polyglot Pipeline

The choice of Quarto (Allaire et al., 2024) as the publishing system was driven by a practical requirement: the substrate simulations are Python (numpy, scipy, executed on the Ray cluster), while the statistical analysis and publication figures are R (ggplot2, arrow, patchwork). Quarto renders both languages in a single .qmd document.

The data interchange format, Apache Parquet, bridges the two ecosystems. Python cluster jobs write per-seed Parquet files; R reads them directly via the arrow package. Parquet’s columnar storage and typed schema prevent the format drift common with CSV interchange. The complete experimental record (approximately 4.6 million session-level observations) compresses to 40 megabytes.

2.4 Distributed Computation

Ray (Moritz et al., 2018) provided the cluster computing layer. A three-node cluster (68 CPUs: 32 on a workstation, 24 on a second node, 12 on the head node) executed all simulation jobs. GEN submitted experiments to the cluster directly from the conversation via Ray’s HTTP job submission API, monitored progress through log polling, and retrieved results by reading Parquet files from a shared NFS mount. When the laptop node disconnected during overnight runs, Ray automatically retried failed tasks on the remaining nodes.

3 The Simulated Committee

Three AI committee members were instantiated from written character descriptions and accumulated meeting transcripts. Each meeting was a fresh agent invocation conditioned on this context, not a persistent process with continuous memory. Within these constraints, the personas produced differentiated intellectual positions:

Dr. Elena Vasquez (Chair, Complex Systems, Santa Fe Institute) — pragmatic, scope-focused, protective of timeline. Made decisive calls on submission timing. Warning against scope creep proved prescient when the literature review expanded the revision plan.

Dr. James Okafor (Computational Neuroscience, UCL) — statistically demanding. Raised the two concerns (pruning confound and CCD invalidity) that reshaped the programme. His insistence on nested ANOVA, Wilcoxon robustness checks, and deconfounded probes improved the statistical foundation.

Dr. Marie-Claire Dupont (Philosophy of Technology, EGS) — philosophically precise. Her correction of “structural germ” to “limit condition” was later validated by an external examiner. Her framing of the “productive fracture” became the programme’s central philosophical contribution.

3.1 Committee Dynamics

The committee’s contribution was structural rather than informational: when members disagreed, the disagreement produced better decisions than any individual perspective. Vasquez’s pragmatism moderated Okafor’s experimental ambition; Dupont’s precision corrected Vasquez’s tendency to dismiss philosophical contributions as inessential.

Positions shifted across meetings because each subsequent invocation included the transcripts of prior meetings as context. Okafor’s partial concession on “phantom individuation,” Vasquez’s shift from leading with the confound to leading with the steepness result, and Dupont’s acceptance that the autopoiesis claim required functional validation were conditioned on prior exchanges, not on persistent memory or genuine intellectual development. Whether this constitutes “evolution” of positions or sophisticated context-matching is an open question.

4 Simulated Peer Review

Each paper submission involved three AI reviewers with distinct expertise, an editorial decision, a response letter, and revision. The process produced genuine quality improvement.

4.1 Issues Identified

Reviewers identified legitimate problems:

  • CCD invalidation was underspecified (Reviewer 1, Paper 4S)
  • The edge cap mechanism potentially reintroduced the pruning confound (Reviewer 2, Paper 4S — described as “the most dangerous item”)
  • “Structural germ” was an incorrect Simondonian category (Reviewer 3, Paper 4S)
  • Critical period and perturbation resilience lacked functional validation (Reviewers 1 and 2, Paper 4)

4.2 Revision Experiments

Addressing reviewer concerns required 900 additional simulation jobs and three new figures. This mirrors real peer review, where revision experiments often exceed the original submission in scope.

4.3 Reviewer Errors

The system was not infallible. One proofreading agent incorrectly flagged verified data as wrong. A reviewer for Paper 4 read the companion Paper 4S and confused cross-paper references. Some questions were answerable from the manuscript itself. These failures are documented because they illustrate the limitations of simulated review.

5 The Literature Review

A parallel deployment of fourteen research agents across five API backends (Claude, Gemini, Grok, Perplexity, and domain-specific contextualizers) found that approximately forty to fifty percent of major results had substantial prior art.

The committee reframed this convergence: independent derivation of established results from an unrelated starting point constitutes evidence that the underlying phenomena are real, not artifacts of any single methodology. The genuinely novel findings — the disordinal interaction, the non-monotonic steepness result, the critical threshold boundaries, and the grid-size scaling law — survived the prior art assessment.

6 The Academic Audit

A coordinating agent executed a seven-phase audit, spawning specialised sub-agents for each verification task:

  1. Citation verification (web search for DOIs and claim accuracy)
  2. Code-data consistency (parquet files against paper claims)
  3. Statistical methods (reproduction of tests from raw values)
  4. Internal consistency (term and number concordance across documents)
  5. Philosophical terminology (correct deployment of Simondonian concepts)
  6. Reproducibility (tracing claim-to-data chains through source code)
  7. Prose quality (logical coherence and argumentative structure)

The audit caught errors that multiple rounds of proofreading had missed: a Shapiro-Wilk p-value reported as 0.40 instead of 0.99, a dissertation framing document with stale terminology and outdated numbers, and a duplicated author name in a bibliography entry.

7 The Mock Defence

Two external examiners were introduced as AI-generated personas (not the actual scholars): “Prof. Randall Beer” (modelled on the Indiana University researcher’s published work in computational autopoiesis) and “Prof. Sara Hooker” (modelled on the Cohere researcher’s published work on compressed network fairness). These names are used to indicate the expertise profile of each persona, not to claim endorsement or participation by the real individuals.

The most difficult exchange concerned whether self-sealing assemblages constitute genuine individuals or merely spatial patterns in a continuous field. The candidate’s response — that the assemblages possess measurable properties and resist perturbation but are constituted by spatial context rather than intrinsic properties — identified the programme’s deepest philosophical vulnerability without fully resolving it.

Verdict: pass with minor corrections.

8 What the System Did Well and Poorly

8.1 Strengths

Parallel execution proved valuable: fourteen literature agents running simultaneously, cluster jobs executing overnight while writing proceeded, multiple committee members debating in a single invocation. Persistent context allowed committee positions to evolve across meetings. The system identified errors during directed investigations: the pruning confound through a human-requested experiment, the CCD invalidity through a human-requested permutation test, and the Shapiro-Wilk miscalculation through a human-requested audit.

8.2 Weaknesses

Literature awareness was insufficient. A researcher familiar with the neural network compression literature would have encountered Hooker et al. before designing the pruning experiments. Metric selection was suboptimal: four papers relied on centroid cosine distance before discovering it was spatially confounded. Philosophical claims occasionally outran the evidence: “structural germ” persisted through several drafts before correction.

8.3 The Role of the Human

One human directed every strategic decision: which questions to pursue, when to run experiments or write, when to consult the committee, when to conduct the literature review, and when to stop. The AI system executed, analysed, wrote, reviewed, and audited. The division was consistent throughout: the human chose the direction; the system travelled it.

9 Implications

9.1 For Research Practice

The committee disagreement format was the most productive architectural choice. Multiple AI personas with distinct intellectual commitments, debating a shared question, produced better outcomes than any individual agent. This suggests that AI-assisted research benefits more from structured disagreement than from optimised individual performance.

9.2 For Academic Integrity

This programme raises questions without settled answers. Who is the author when writing is AI-generated under human direction? How should simulated peer review be disclosed? When an AI system independently derives a result with prior art, is the appropriate response citation of the prior work, acknowledgment of independent derivation, or both? This document exists to make the process transparent so that others can evaluate it on its merits.

9.3 For Reproducibility

Every artifact in the accompanying repository — code, data, papers, committee transcripts, peer reviews, audit reports, and this document — is provided so that the process can be examined, critiqued, and, if desired, replicated.
