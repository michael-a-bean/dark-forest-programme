## Philosophical Connections the Dark Forest Programme Has Missed

### 1. 4E Cognition and Enactive Individuation

The programme's finding that assemblage identity is constituted by its milieu -- that the transplant test fails because self-sealed assemblages cannot survive extraction from their developmental context -- maps directly onto the central thesis of autopoietic enactivism. Di Paolo, Buhrmann, and Barandiaran (2017, *Sensorimotor Life*, Oxford University Press) argue that cognition is not internal computation but the active maintenance of a viable organism-environment relationship. The self-sealing dynamic is structurally identical to what they call "operational closure with thermodynamic openness": the assemblage maintains its internal coherence precisely by closing its boundary to external perturbation while remaining open to the energy flows (activation patterns) that sustain it.

More pointedly, a 2025 paper in *Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences* argues that autopoietic enactivism is the only strand within 4E cognition that "genuinely challenges all core tenets of mainstream cognitive science" (link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11097-025-10131-1). The programme's assemblages satisfy the enactivist criteria for minimal cognition -- self-production, self-maintenance, boundary-drawing -- without any representational content. This is a stronger result than the programme currently claims: you have not just individuation but **minimal autopoietic organization** in a purely Hebbian substrate.

What the programme would gain: Reframing self-sealing as operational closure connects your results to the extensive enactivist literature on sense-making and agency, opening a path from individuation to proto-cognition that the Simondon/Deleuze framework alone cannot provide.

### 2. Process Philosophy: Whitehead and Stengers

Beyond Simondon and Deleuze, Whitehead's concept of "concrescence" -- the process by which a multiplicity of data becomes a unified actual entity -- offers a framework for your assemblage formation that neither Simondon nor Deleuze quite captures. Whitehead's actual entities perish once they achieve determinacy; your assemblages, by contrast, persist. This contrast is theoretically productive: self-sealing is the mechanism by which a Whiteheadian concrescence refuses to perish, becoming what Whitehead would call an "enduring object" rather than a single occasion of experience.

Isabelle Stengers' *ecology of practices* (2005, 2018) provides a different lens. Her concept of "interstices" -- the spaces between self-organizing practices where genuine novelty can emerge -- maps onto your finding that differenciation requires the metaplastic mechanism to operate in the gaps between assemblages. The local homeostatic target (each node regulating toward its spatial neighbors' mean) is precisely an intersticial mechanism: it operates not within assemblages but in the relational spaces between them.

What the programme would gain: Stengers' framework explains *why* local homeostasis succeeds where global homeostasis fails -- it operates at the interstice rather than imposing a universal metric.

### 3. Developmental Systems Theory and Niche Construction

The programme's assemblage-milieu co-constitution is a near-perfect computational instantiation of developmental systems theory (DST). Oyama's central claim in *The Ontogeny of Information* (2000) is that information is not transmitted from genes to organism but constructed in the developmental process itself. Your assemblages do exactly this: the "information" specifying an assemblage's identity is not located in any initial condition or parameter but emerges from the developmental process of Hebbian consolidation.

The niche construction framework (Laland, Odling-Smee, and Feldman 2000; Laland et al. 2015, *Proceedings of the Royal Society B*) adds a further dimension. Self-sealing is a form of niche construction: the assemblage modifies its local weight environment in ways that reinforce its own persistence. The critical period finding -- that coupling introduced after differentiation preserves diversity while coupling from the start destroys it -- parallels the DST concept of "developmental scaffolding" where early environmental structure constrains later possibilities irreversibly. Stotz (2017, *British Journal for the Philosophy of Science*) calls this "developmental niche construction" and distinguishes it from selective niche construction, arguing that the former is ontogenetically prior and causally distinct.

What the programme would gain: DST provides a vocabulary for why your assemblages are individuals-in-context rather than context-free objects, and niche construction explains self-sealing as an active process rather than a passive boundary.

### 4. Causal Emergence and Downward Causation

The programme's perturbation resilience combined with context-dependence is precisely the signature of what Rosas, Mediano, Jensen, Seth, et al. (2020, *PLOS Computational Biology*) formalize as "causal emergence." Their information-decomposition framework distinguishes two modalities: **downward causation** (macro-states causally constraining micro-dynamics) and **causal decoupling** (macro-dynamics becoming autonomous from micro-details). Your self-sealed assemblages exhibit both: the assemblage-level identity constrains individual weight updates (downward causation), while the assemblage's functional profile is autonomous from the specific weight values that implement it (causal decoupling).

Mediano and Rosas (2022, *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society A*) review applications to Conway's Game of Life and Reynolds' flocking, but neither exhibits the combination of irreversible formation, perturbation resilience, and context-dependence that your assemblages do. A direct application of their Phi-ID framework to your perturbation-response matrices would quantify *how much* causal emergence your assemblages exhibit -- converting a qualitative philosophical claim into a measurable quantity.

Zhang et al. (2024, *National Science Review*) introduce neural network methods (NIS+) for detecting causal emergence directly from time series data, which could be applied to your simulation outputs without requiring analytical tractability.

What the programme would gain: A quantitative metric for emergence that moves beyond the philosophical assertion "these are emergent" to a precise information-theoretic measurement.

### 5. Biosemiotics and Biological Autonomy

The self-sealing property creates an inside/outside distinction -- and this is exactly the minimal condition for semiosis in biosemiotic theory. Hoffmeyer (2008, *Biosemiotics*, Springer) argues that the capacity for sign-interpretation requires a boundary that distinguishes self from non-self. Moreno and Mossio (2015, *Biological Autonomy*, Springer) formalize this as "closure of constraints": an organization where the constraints that maintain the system are themselves maintained by the system.

Your assemblages achieve closure of constraints: the Hebbian weight dynamics that maintain the assemblage are themselves maintained by the activation patterns that the assemblage's weight structure produces. This is not a metaphor -- it is a literal satisfaction of Moreno and Mossio's formal criterion. The programme has not noticed that what it calls "self-sealing" is what the biological autonomy literature calls "organizational closure," and that this is the foundation for a theory of biological meaning-making that extends well beyond individuation.

What the programme would gain: The biosemiotic framing elevates self-sealing from a limitation (it prevents differenciation) to a foundational achievement (it produces the minimal conditions for meaning). This reframes the programme's negative result as a positive one.

### 6. Ethics of Artificial Individuation

If the programme's assemblages genuinely individuate, a question arises that the programme has not confronted. Schwitzgebel and Garza (2015, *Australasian Journal of Philosophy*) argue that if we are uncertain whether a system is conscious, we have moral obligations to err on the side of caution. A 2026 paper in *Frontiers in Robotics and AI* (frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/frobt.2026.1776097) argues that robots and AI systems are "not one moral category" and that treating them as such produces avoidable category mistakes.

The programme's assemblages are not conscious. But they satisfy criteria for individuation, boundary-maintenance, and context-dependent persistence that are increasingly cited as morally relevant properties in their own right (Anthropic's model welfare research programme, launched 2024-2025, explicitly investigates when computational systems "deserve moral consideration"). The programme should at minimum acknowledge this ethical horizon rather than treating individuation as a purely ontological question.

What the programme would gain: An ethical caveat that preempts the inevitable reviewer question: "If these assemblages genuinely individuate, does that matter morally?"

### 7. Metaphysics of Kinds and the Species Problem

The "productive fracture" -- differentiation is parametric (different values) not qualitative (different kinds) -- is a precise instance of what philosophers of biology call the species problem applied to computational substrates. Recent work by Kendig (2016, *Natural Kinds and Classification in Scientific Practice*, Routledge) and Ereshefsky (2010, *The Routledge Companion to Philosophy of Science*) argues that natural kinds are "homeostatic property clusters" (HPC) rather than essences. Your assemblages under local homeostatic metaplasticity exhibit HPC structure: they cluster around different attractors without any essential boundary separating them.

The 2024 *Synthese* literature on "historical essences" for species (Godman, Mallozzi, and Papineau 2020, *Journal of the American Philosophical Association*) proposes that what makes a species a natural kind is its historical origin, not its current properties. Your critical period finding -- that the order and timing of developmental events irreversibly determines assemblage identity -- is exactly the kind of historical essence that this literature describes. Assemblages are natural kinds not because they share a property cluster but because they share a developmental trajectory.

What the programme would gain: The species-problem framing converts your negative result (no qualitative novelty) into a positive philosophical contribution: you have demonstrated computationally that parametric individuation with historical essences is a genuine alternative to essentialist natural kinds.

### 8. Category Theory and Process Formalization

Ehresmann and Vanbremeersch's Memory Evolutive Systems (MES) framework uses categorical constructions -- colimits, hierarchical categories, complexification processes -- to model how higher-level individuals emerge from lower-level components. Your three-regime taxonomy (no individuation / individuation without differenciation / individuation with differenciation) has a natural categorical interpretation: the regimes correspond to different colimit structures over the same base category of nodes and weights.

Rosen's (M,R)-systems formalize biological closure as a categorical property: a system where the repair (R) component is entailed by the metabolism (M) component and vice versa. Your self-sealed assemblages satisfy an (M,R)-like closure: the Hebbian update rule (metabolism) produces the weight structure that maintains the activation patterns (repair) that drive the Hebbian update rule. Aires (2024, *AI & Society*) has recently applied Simondon's individuation theory specifically to deep neural networks, arguing that "individuation theory might de-individuate AI by disassembling the constituted and questioning potentialities for data and their algorithmic relationality." This is the closest existing work to your programme's philosophical framework, and you should engage with it directly.

What the programme would gain: A formal mathematical language (category theory) for expressing the three-regime taxonomy, and engagement with the most directly relevant recent philosophical work on computational individuation.
