8  Synthesis

8.1 Convergent Frameworks Across Scales

Several of the programme’s findings converge with results established independently in other fields. This convergence is not a weakness — it is evidence that the phenomena identified are real and domain-general.

Pruning and functional diversity. The finding that pruning destroys functional diversity (Paper 4S) converges with the ML compression literature: Hooker et al. (2019) showed compressed networks disproportionately forget rare classes; Tran et al. (NeurIPS 2022) identified gradient-norm asymmetry as the mechanism; Holste et al. (MICCAI 2023) demonstrated clinical consequences. Across six non-neural domains — metallurgy (grain boundaries), ecology (mass extinction), markets (killer acquisitions), supply chains (redundancy removal), immune systems (thymic selection), and social networks (Granovetter’s weak ties) — the same pattern holds: removing “weak” elements collapses the system’s functional response dimensionality.

Neural dimensionality. The response rank metric (SVD participation ratio) is the standard dimensionality measure in systems neuroscience (Stringer et al. 2019; Gao et al. 2017; Rigotti et al. 2013). Our independent derivation of the same metric confirms its appropriateness. The finding that pruning reduces rank from 5.6 to 1.0 is a testable prediction for experimental neuroscience: does developmental synaptic pruning reduce the participation ratio of cortical population responses?

Critical periods. Emergent critical periods have been demonstrated in deep networks (Achille et al. 2019; Kleinman et al. 2024). Our substrate produces them through a different mechanism — structural consolidation rather than information-theoretic sensitivity — but the convergence confirms that critical periods are a fundamental property of hierarchical learning systems, not specific to biological neurons.

Operational closure. The self-sealing property (Paper 3) is structurally equivalent to operational closure in autopoietic theory (Maturana & Varela 1980). We note this connection without claiming full autopoietic organisation: the assemblages are operationally closed but not self-producing (Beer 2020).

Open-endedness. The “productive fracture” (parametric differentiation without qualitative novelty) maps onto the open-ended evolution literature. Adams et al. (2017) demonstrated formally that only state-dependent update rules produce unbounded evolution. Our fixed Hebbian rule produces bounded dynamics by the Poincaré recurrence argument. The path forward — rule-space expansion — is the same conclusion reached independently by the Flow-Lenia programme (Plantec et al. 2023).

The general principle. Across all domains, one finding generalises: maintenance operations in self-organising systems interact with the phenomena they are supposed to maintain, and this interaction can be disordinal — reversing the apparent ranking of approaches. This specific result (the ranking reversal) has no prior art in any domain we surveyed.

8.2 Cross-Cutting Themes

8.2.1 The role of boundary conditions

A recurring finding: boundary conditions are not implementation details.

  • The hard weight clip creates qualitatively different dynamics from smooth saturation (Paper 4S). No finite steepness of soft saturation recovers hard clip behaviour.
  • The pruning threshold functions as an unintended mould, selectively eliminating soft-bounded assemblages (Paper 4S).
  • The \(\eta\) bounds (hard vs soft) determine whether metaplastic differentiation occurs (Paper 4).

In each case, what appears to be a neutral parameter choice shapes the system’s individuating capacity. Simondon’s framework predicts this: individuation proceeds from singularities in the pre-individual field, and boundary conditions are precisely such singularities.

8.2.2 Self-organisation requires constraint

The programme demonstrates, against a naive reading of Deleuze, that individuation requires boundary conditions. The fully continuous substrate (soft weights, soft \(\eta\), no pruning) produces topological structure (6–8 assemblages) but not functional differentiation (response rank \(1\)–\(2\)). Functional differentiation requires the absorbing boundary of hard clipping (rank \(5.6\)). The constraint is not an imposition of form — it is the condition under which the system can resolve its own metastability.

This is consistent with Simondon’s analysis: modulation is the more general operation, but moulding (a limit case of modulation) produces the strongest individuation. The hard clip is productive precisely because it forecloses certain dynamics, channelling the system’s energy into fewer, more distinct configurations.

8.2.3 Negative results as contributions

Three negative results proved essential:

  1. Paper 3’s finding that self-sealing prevents differentiation established the barrier that motivated Paper 4’s metaplastic mechanism.
  2. Paper 4S’s finding that CCD is spatially confounded forced the adoption of response rank as the primary metric, yielding a more rigorous measure of functional differentiation.
  3. Paper 4S’s null response-profile results under Gaussian probes led to the discovery that spatially structured probes deflate response rank by creating correlated activation across assemblages.

Each negative result redirected the programme toward a stronger positive finding.

8.2.4 The three regimes of individuation

The complete experimental record supports a taxonomy of three nested dynamical regimes:

Regime Assemblages Response rank \(\eta\) F-ratio Bound type
Topological 6–8 1–2 \(<1\) Soft, no pruning
Structural 5–6 5.6 — Hard, no pruning
Metaplastic 10+ 5.6 4.9 Hard, no pruning, local homeostasis

These are three phases of the same Hebbian process observed under different boundary conditions. Each subsumes the previous: structural individuation requires the hard clip, metaplastic individuation requires both the hard clip and local homeostasis.

8.2.5 The productive fracture

The programme identifies a precise boundary between Simondonian individuation and Deleuzian differenciation. The Hebbian substrate achieves the former readily and the latter only partially. Even the metaplastic regime produces parametric differentiation — different values of the same capacities — not qualitative differenciation — new kinds of dynamics.

If differenciation in the strong Deleuzian sense requires the production of genuinely novel capacities, then Hebbian substrates require rule-space expansion: mechanisms that generate new kinds of update rules, not just distribute existing ones across space. This points toward substrate architectures with evolvable learning rules as the next step.
