9  Conclusion

9.1 Summary of Contributions

The Dark Forest programme demonstrates that functional differentiation in self-organising networks emerges from the interaction of four elements: spatial structure, Hebbian reinforcement, absorbing weight boundaries, and local homeostatic regulation. Remove any one and the system produces a less complex form of organisation.

9.1.1 Empirical contributions

  1. Individuation is easy; differentiation is hard. Pure Hebbian dynamics produce spontaneous assemblage formation (Paper 1), and encounter locality governs a sharp coexistence–monopoly phase transition (Paper 2). But assemblages are structural clones, and the substrate is self-sealing against every intervention within the Hebbian framework (Paper 3).

  2. Local homeostasis breaks the barrier. Per-node learning rate adjustment toward local activity targets produces stable functional differentiation (response rank \(5.6\), \(\eta\) divergence \(F = 4.9\)) sustained over 1000 sessions with critical period dynamics (Paper 4).

  3. Pruning suppresses the signal. The standard pruning threshold differentially eliminates soft-bounded edges, creating a disordinal interaction with weight bounding strategy that distorted the programme’s central comparisons. Removing pruning reveals substantially stronger functional diversity than originally measured (Paper 4S).

  4. The hard clip is qualitatively irreducible. No finite steepness of soft saturation recovers hard clip behaviour. The hard boundary functions as a Simondonian limit condition that scaffolds functional diversity (Paper 4S).

9.1.2 Methodological contributions

  1. CCD is spatially confounded. Centroid cosine distance, widely used in Hebbian network analysis, is indistinguishable from a spatial null distribution. Response rank (SVD effective dimensionality) is the appropriate functional metric.

  2. Maintenance operations interact with self-organisation. Any computational model that removes weak connections may be destroying the exploratory structure that enables functional diversity. This finding generalises beyond the specific programme.

9.1.3 Philosophical contributions

  1. Individuation without differenciation. The programme provides an experimental demonstration that Simondonian individuation and Deleuzian differenciation come apart. The Hebbian substrate achieves the former readily; the latter requires specific structural conditions (absorbing boundaries, local regulation) and even then remains parametric.

  2. The productive fracture. The gap between differentiation (achieved) and differenciation (not achieved) is precisely characterised as the absence of rule-space expansion. This points toward what a next-generation substrate would require.

9.2 Limitations

The programme studies a specific substrate architecture (2D grid, Hebbian learning, hard weight clipping) and the findings may not generalise to all self-organising systems. The response rank metric, while an improvement over CCD, measures functional diversity within the substrate’s existing dynamics and cannot detect capacities outside its vocabulary. The philosophical framework, while rigorously applied, involves interpretive choices that other readings of Simondon and Deleuze might contest.

The 12% assemblage yield for the eta ANOVA condition (6/50 seeds) indicates that multi-assemblage coexistence under hard weight + soft \(\eta\) bounds is a rare dynamical regime. The effect is robust when present but does not represent the typical outcome.

All experiments in the programme use regular two-dimensional lattices with von Neumann or distance-weighted connectivity. The coexistence–monopoly phase transition, the self-sealing timeline, the critical period dynamics, the steepness non-monotonicity, and the pruning confound may all be topology-dependent. The grid-size scaling law (the pruning interaction amplifies from \(\Delta = 4.2\) at \(15 \times 15\) to \(\Delta = 14.5\) at \(30 \times 30\)) confirms that spatial scale matters but does not establish topology-independence. Testing on small-world, scale-free, and random geometric graph topologies is essential future work. The local homeostatic mechanism, which depends on spatial neighbours, may behave qualitatively differently when “local” is defined by graph distance rather than Euclidean distance on a lattice.

9.3 Broader Implications

9.3.1 Neurodevelopmental disorders

The pruning-diversity interaction connects directly to the pruning-schizophrenia hypothesis. Sekar et al. (2016, Nature) identified complement component C4 gene variants as schizophrenia risk factors through their control of synaptic pruning rate. Yilmaz et al. (2021, Nature Neuroscience) confirmed that C4A overexpression reduces cortical synapse density. Our critical period finding (Exp 8fv) predicts that the timing of pruning relative to consolidation matters more than the amount — excessive pruning during the critical period should be more damaging than the same pruning after consolidation. This is a testable clinical prediction.

9.3.2 Neural network compression

The ML pruning fairness literature (Hooker et al. 2019; Tran et al. 2022) documents that compressed networks disproportionately lose rare-class performance. Our response rank metric could diagnose this as dimensionality collapse: measure the participation ratio of layer-wise responses before and after pruning. If pruned networks show rank collapse analogous to our substrate, this provides a mechanistic explanation and suggests pruning strategies that preserve representational diversity.

9.3.3 Immune repertoire maintenance

T-cell homeostatic proliferation operates through mechanisms structurally isomorphic to our local homeostatic targets: clones compete in spatially structured niches, regulate toward local carrying capacity, and maintain diversity through hard activation thresholds. Immunosuppressive therapy destroying rare clonotypes parallels our pruning destroying weak assemblages. The programme’s framework could inform models of immune repertoire maintenance and predict conditions under which therapeutic pruning destroys protective diversity.

9.3.4 The general principle

Across every domain examined — neural networks, metallurgy, ecology, markets, supply chains, immune systems — removing weak elements from self-organising systems collapses functional diversity. The elements that single-metric optimisation identifies as “weak” are disproportionately the ones carrying inter-unit coupling that enables multi-dimensional response. This is a domain-general principle with the programme’s disordinal interaction as its most precise computational demonstration.

9.4 Future Directions

9.4.1 Rule-space expansion

The programme’s central finding — that differentiation remains parametric — points directly toward the next research question. A substrate with evolvable learning rules, where assemblages can develop qualitatively different update dynamics, could bridge the gap between differentiation and differenciation. Candidates include neural network architectures with learnable activation functions, meta-learning systems, or developmental programmes with conditional gene expression analogs.

9.4.2 Pruning as a research programme

The discovery that pruning interacts with self-organisation opens a broader investigation. Biological synaptic pruning during development is widely modelled as constructive (improving signal-to-noise ratio). The Dark Forest results suggest it may also be destructive for functional diversity. An experimental programme systematically varying pruning strategies (threshold-based, rank-based, activity-dependent, random) across different substrates could characterise this interaction in generality.

9.4.3 Scaling

The grid-size scaling law (Paper 4S, \(\Delta\) increasing from \(4.2\) at \(15 \times 15\) to \(14.5\) at \(30 \times 30\)) suggests the pruning confound is more severe at larger scales. Whether the functional differentiation results (response rank \(5.6\)) scale similarly is an open question with implications for biologically realistic network sizes.

9.4.4 Non-lattice topologies

The programme’s substrate uses a regular 2D grid. Whether the findings — particularly the local homeostatic mechanism and the critical period — transfer to random geometric graphs, small-world networks, or scale-free topologies is unknown and would test the generality of the results.

9.5 Closing

Self-organising networks on spatially structured substrates spontaneously produce functionally diverse assemblages through Hebbian reinforcement alone, but standard network maintenance systematically destroys this diversity by selectively removing the weak exploratory connections that distinguish one assemblage from another. Metaplasticity stabilises and amplifies this diversity. The result is a minimal model of how individuation emerges from homogeneous substrates: not through any single mechanism, but through the interaction of spatial structure, reinforcement dynamics, and adaptive learning rules, provided the maintenance regime does not destroy the evidence.
