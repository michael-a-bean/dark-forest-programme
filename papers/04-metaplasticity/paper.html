Local Homeostasis Stabilises Differentiation in Hebbian Substrates

Critical Periods, Attractor Resilience, and the Limits of Turing Morphogenesis

Author
Affiliation

Michael Bean

Independent Researcher

Abstract

Papers 1–3 of this programme demonstrated that pure Hebbian substrates produce individuation—assemblages emerge spontaneously from noise—but are structurally incapable of differenciation1: self-sealing eliminates the prerequisites for lateral inhibition and inter-unit communication. Here we test the theoretically motivated hypothesis that a Turing morphogenesis analog (per-node metaplastic learning rate + diffusing inhibitor field) can break this barrier. Under hard \(\eta\) bounds, the Turing mechanism fails completely: inhibitor coupling is purely destructive regardless of sign, strength, or coupling topology. Under soft \(\eta\) bounds, coupling is partially rescued but remains inferior to a simpler mechanism. Replacing the global homeostatic target with a local target—each node regulates toward its spatial neighbors’ mean activity—transforms the system without requiring any inter-assemblage communication. Local homeostatic metaplasticity stabilises and amplifies functional differentiation among initially identical assemblages, sustained over 1000 sessions with no decay. A perturbation-response matrix protocol reveals that assemblages develop genuinely independent functional dimensions: response rank reaches \(5.6\) (over five independent response modes) under hard bounds without pruning, compared to \(\approx 1.2\) under standard maintenance (Paper 4S). The metaplastic mechanism adds learning-rate divergence between assemblages (\(F = 4.9\), \(p < 0.0001\), \(\eta^2_p = 0.83\)), producing \(10+\) assemblages with distinct functional profiles. The system exhibits critical period dynamics: inter-assemblage coupling introduced after differentiation preserves diversity, while coupling from the start destroys it. We interpret these findings through a triple philosophical framework: Simondon’s transduction explains the mechanism, Deleuze’s ontology of problems explains why differentiation occurs, and DeLanda’s capacity criterion distinguishes genuine functional differentiation from mere spatial separation. The work identifies a productive fracture: the hard weight clip functions as a limit condition in Simondon’s sense—a qualitatively irreducible boundary condition that creates the scaffold for metaplastic differentiation (Paper 4S).

Introduction

The question motivating this programme is deceptively simple: what is the minimal substrate that produces genuine functional differentiation? Not mere spatial separation, not clonal repetition, but the emergence of qualitatively different kinds from initially identical components.

Papers 1–3 established the baseline. A Hebbian substrate—nodes on a 2D grid connected by sparse, activity-dependent weights updated by a standard Hebbian rule—spontaneously produces individuation: coherent assemblages emerge from noise, consolidate through self-reinforcing weight dynamics, and persist as distinct entities (Watson and Szathmáry 2016). But every assemblage is a structural clone of every other. They differ in spatial position but not in internal organisation, connectivity profile, or functional capacity.

The mechanism responsible is self-sealing. As assemblages consolidate, they strengthen internal connections and prune external ones. The self-reinforcing Hebbian dynamic creates an absorbing boundary: once sealed, no local perturbation can reopen the assemblage to external influence. Paper 3 tested every intervention available within the Hebbian framework—anti-Hebbian learning, cross-assemblage edge injection, temporal variation of the energy landscape—and all failed. Self-sealing eliminates the two prerequisites for differenciation identified by the pattern formation literature: lateral inhibition and inter-unit communication (Turing 1952; Gierer and Meinhardt 1972).

In the Deleuzian framework that structures this programme, self-sealing corresponds to bare repetition: the same intensive process applied identically at multiple locations produces identical copies. Complex repetition—differenciation—requires that the process itself change, producing genuinely novel structures rather than spatial duplicates (Deleuze 1994; DeLanda 2002).

The natural hypothesis, then, is a Turing morphogenesis analog. If Hebbian reinforcement acts as a short-range activator and a diffusing inhibitor field acts as the long-range inhibitor, Turing instability at the metaplastic level—modifying the learning rules themselves rather than the weights directly—might break the symmetry that self-sealing preserves.

This paper tests that hypothesis and finds a more complex answer than anticipated. Under hard \(\eta\) bounds, the Turing mechanism fails completely for structural reasons consistent with the network pattern formation literature (Wolfrum 2012; Nakao and Mikhailov 2010; Krause et al. 2024). Under soft \(\eta\) bounds, coupling is partially rescued, confirming that the initial failure was partly an implementation artifact (hard \(\eta\) clipping violates the continuity of intensive quantities that the Deleuzian framework assumes). But even with soft bounds, coupling remains inferior to a simpler, more robust mechanism: local homeostatic targets that produce stable differentiation without any inter-assemblage communication. The critical result is that communication becomes constructive only after differentiation has been established, exhibiting critical period dynamics analogous to those in cortical development (Bienenstock, Cooper, and Munro 1982; Sweeney et al. 2015). A functional probe confirms that the resulting differentiation is not merely geometric: assemblages develop distinct signal amplification profiles, partially satisfying DeLanda’s criterion for differentiation of capacities (DeLanda 2006). This stabilisation is contingent on the network maintenance regime: the companion Paper 4S demonstrates that the standard pruning threshold (\(10^{-4}\)) differentially eliminates soft-bounded edges, and that removing pruning substantially increases the measured functional diversity (see Methods).

Methods

Substrate

The MetaplasticField extends the MultiField substrate from Paper 2. Nodes are arranged on a 2D grid (default 20 \(\times\) 20 = 400 nodes). Energy sources (hotspots) drive spatially varying noise injection. Weights are sparse, updated by the Hebbian rule, and pruned below a threshold. Activity-dependent encounters create new connections biased toward spatial neighbours, governed by a locality parameter.

Methodological note on pruning. All experiments in this paper use a pruning threshold of \(10^{-4}\): edges with \(|w| < 10^{-4}\) are removed each step. Paper 4S demonstrates that this threshold interacts disordinally with weight bounding strategy, differentially eliminating soft-bounded edges whose equilibrium magnitudes fall near the threshold. Under hard weight bounds (the only bound type used in this paper’s primary analyses), the pruning threshold has minimal effect on assemblage counts (3.9 with pruning vs 5.6 without; see Paper 4S, Exp F). However, no-pruning replications reveal that functional differentiation (response rank) increases substantially from 1.3 to 5.6 when pruning is removed (Paper 4S, 10-probe methodology). The results reported here should therefore be understood as conservative estimates of the substrate’s differentiating capacity. See Paper 4S for full analysis.

Metaplastic learning rate

Each node \(i\) maintains its own learning rate \(\eta_i\), initialised identically to the global \(\eta = 0.05\). The Hebbian update for edge \((i, j)\) uses the average: \(\eta_{ij} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\eta_i + \eta_j)\).

A homeostatic adjustment modifies \(\eta_i\) each step:

\[\Delta\eta_i = -\alpha \cdot (\bar{a}_i - \tau_i)\]

where \(\alpha\) is the metaplastic strength, \(\bar{a}_i\) is the exponential moving average of activity history (default rate \(= 0.05\), i.e. 95% old + 5% new, following the conventional value used in homeostatic plasticity models (Sweeney et al. 2015); Exp 18 confirms robustness across an order of magnitude), and \(\tau_i\) is the homeostatic target.

Two target regimes are compared:

  • Global target (original): \(\tau_i = 0.15\) for all nodes.
  • Local target (corrective): \(\tau_i = \text{mean}(\bar{a}_j : j \in \mathcal{N}_r(i))\), where \(\mathcal{N}_r(i)\) is the set of grid neighbours within Manhattan radius \(r\).

Learning rates are bounded: \(\eta_i \in [0.005, 0.20]\). Two bounding regimes are compared: hard clipping (\(\eta_i\) set to the nearer bound when exceeded) and soft bounding (tanh saturation that asymptotically approaches but never reaches the bounds). Weights are always hard-clipped at \(w_\text{max}\) regardless of \(\eta\)-bounding regime. This distinction matters: hard \(\eta\) clipping creates absorbing states where 40% of nodes accumulate at the bounds, producing a bimodal distribution of learning rates. Soft bounding preserves a continuous, unimodal distribution. The synaptic plasticity literature has established that this hard/soft distinction produces qualitatively different dynamics: hard-bounded STDP drives weights to bimodal distributions, while weight-dependent (soft) rules produce stable unimodal distributions (Rossum, Bi, and Turrigiano 2000; Miller and MacKay 1994; Song, Miller, and Abbott 2000).

Inhibitor field

Nodes emit inhibitor proportional to their total weight mass. The inhibitor diffuses via a discrete Laplacian (coefficient \(\leq 0.20\) for CFL stability), decays multiplicatively each step, and modulates \(\eta\) via a coupling term. Two coupling regimes were tested:

  • Upward (original): inhibitor pushes \(\eta\) up.
  • Downward + difference-dependent (corrective): inhibitor suppresses \(\eta\) proportional to the difference between the node’s \(\eta\) and its neighbours’ mean \(\eta\).

Metrics

Primary functional metrics:

  • Response rank: effective rank of the perturbation-response matrix (SVD, threshold at 1% of largest singular value). Rank \(= 1\) indicates parametric differentiation (assemblages scale inputs identically); rank \(> 1\) indicates genuinely functional differentiation (assemblages transform inputs through distinct mappings). This is the gold-standard measure of capacity differentiation.
  • Response diversity: standard deviation of assemblage gains (gain \(= \bar{\eta} \times \bar{w}_\text{internal}\)), a proxy for signal amplification capacity.
  • \(\eta\) divergence: variance of per-assemblage mean \(\eta\).

Structural metrics:

  • Centroid cosine distance (CCD): mean pairwise cosine distance between assemblage centroid weight vectors. 0 = clones, 1 = orthogonal. Paper 4S demonstrates that CCD is statistically indistinguishable from a spatial null distribution (\(p = 1.0\)); it reflects geometric separation, not functional differentiation. CCD values reported here should be interpreted as spatial structure measures only.
  • Weight modularity: fraction of total weight mass within assemblages.
  • Assemblage count: connected components in the strong-edge subgraph.

Experiments 1–3 tested the Turing hypothesis with the original mechanism (global homeostatic target, upward inhibitor coupling).

Metaplasticity alone (Exp 1) produces monopoly at \(\alpha \geq 0.0005\): all assemblages consolidate into a single mega-assemblage. The global target (\(\tau = 0.15\)) crushes \(\eta\) toward \(\eta_\text{min}\) for all assemblage nodes (activity \(\approx 0.85 \gg 0.15\)), producing identical homeostatic adjustments across assemblages. The first assemblage to consolidate wins.

Communication alone (Exp 2) pushes all \(\eta\) to \(\eta_\text{max}\) uniformly. Without metaplasticity creating heterogeneous \(\eta\), the inhibitor acts as a uniform positive pressure with no differences to amplify.

The combination (Exp 3) produces no synergy. The best parameters are \(\alpha = 0\), coupling \(= 0\)—the baseline with both mechanisms off.

The corrective experiment (Exp 5) applied three fixes identified through mechanistic analysis: sign reversal (inhibitor suppresses \(\eta\)), local homeostatic target, and difference-dependent coupling. Under hard \(\eta\) bounds, inhibitor coupling remained purely destructive at every parameter combination, reducing centroid distance relative to the coupling-free condition.

However, the soft-bounds experiment (Exp 11) reveals that this destruction is partly artifactual. When hard \(\eta\) clipping is replaced with soft \(\eta\) bounding (tanh saturation), coupling exhibits the U-shaped dose-response predicted by Turing theory: low coupling (\(c \leq 0.001\)) is mildly destructive, but strong coupling (\(c \geq 0.02\)) becomes constructive. At \(\alpha = 0.05\), \(c = 0.05\), centroid cosine distance rises from 0.00 (hard bounds) to 0.65 (soft bounds)—a qualitative reversal.

The mechanism involves \(\eta\) bounding, not weight clipping (weights are always hard-clipped). Under hard \(\eta\) bounds, the homeostatic adjustment pushes learning rates to the floor (\(\eta_\text{min}\)) for high-activity nodes and the ceiling (\(\eta_\text{max}\)) for low-activity nodes. Once pinned, \(\eta\) cannot respond to further coupling signals—the learning rate is informationally dead at the bound. Soft \(\eta\) bounding (tanh saturation) preserves responsiveness: \(\eta\) asymptotically approaches but never reaches the limits, maintaining the continuous modulation that reaction-diffusion dynamics require. This parallels a well-established finding in the STDP literature: hard-bounded (additive) update rules drive synaptic variables to bimodal distributions concentrated at the bounds, while weight-dependent (multiplicative/soft) rules produce stable unimodal distributions (Rossum, Bi, and Turrigiano 2000; Song, Miller, and Abbott 2000; Rubin, Lee, and Sompolinsky 2001). Our finding extends this principle from the synaptic level to the metaplastic level.

Nevertheless, even at its best, soft-bounds coupling (CCD \(= 0.65\)) remains below local homeostasis without coupling (CCD \(= 0.84\) under hard bounds). Note: Paper 4S demonstrates that CCD is statistically indistinguishable from a spatial null distribution (\(p = 1.0\), 1000 permutations) and reflects geometric separation rather than functional differentiation. The CCD values reported here should be interpreted as measures of spatial assemblage separation, not as evidence of functional differentiation per se. The functional differentiation evidence rests on response diversity and response rank (see below and Paper 4S, Exp 17np). Two structural arguments against Turing patterns on this substrate persist, consistent with the network Turing literature. Wolfrum (2012) showed that Turing bifurcations on sparse networks generically produce single-differentiated-node solutions rather than distributed patterns. Krause et al. (2024) proved that satisfying Turing instability conditions is insufficient to guarantee sustained pattern formation. Nakao and Mikhailov (2010) established that network Turing patterns are governed by the Laplacian eigenspectrum, which in sparse graphs supports only binary differentiation. Our substrate compounds these difficulties: the topology dynamically rewires under Hebbian learning, making the diffusion matrix non-stationary.

The critical finding emerges from a single modification: replacing the global homeostatic target with a local one. At \(\alpha = 0.05\), the global target produces monopoly (\(n_\text{asm} \approx 1\)), while the local target produces \(5.2\) differentiated assemblages with centroid cosine distance \(0.84\) [0.81, 0.87] and \(\eta\) divergence \(0.003\).

The mechanism: with a global target, every assemblage node receives an identical homeostatic adjustment. With a local target, each node’s reference point is its own neighbourhood’s activity. Nodes inside assemblages are surrounded by other active nodes—their target is high, their \(\eta\) barely adjusts. Nodes at assemblage boundaries see a mix of activity levels—their \(\eta\) adjusts differently. Different spatial positions create different effective problems, breaking the symmetry that produces clones.

This maps precisely onto Sweeney et al. (2015), who showed that diffusive homeostasis maintains heterogeneous firing rate distributions in cortical networks, while non-diffusive (global) homeostasis produces narrow, homogeneous distributions.

Experiment 8 is the decisive test of whether communication is fundamentally incompatible with differentiation in this substrate. The answer is nuanced: timing determines everything.

Coupling introduced at session 0 reduces centroid cosine distance from \(0.84\) [0.81, 0.87] to \(0.07\) [0.05, 0.10] at the strongest coupling (\(c = 0.01\)). Coupling delayed to session 50 preserves differentiation at \(0.74\) [0.70, 0.78] even at \(c = 0.01\). By session 100, the effect of coupling is indistinguishable from the coupling-free condition (\(0.78\) [0.74, 0.82]).

This is a critical period in the developmental sense (Bienenstock, Cooper, and Munro 1982): early plasticity establishes structure, and premature constraint destroys it. The assemblages must consolidate their internal organisation before external coupling can be tolerated. Once consolidated, the coupling encounters basins of attraction that are already carved—it navigates within a structured landscape rather than homogenising a flat one.

Functional validation (Exp 8fv). A replication of Exp 8 using 10-probe response rank (SVD, 1% threshold) confirms the critical period holds for functional diversity, not merely geometric separation. At coupling \(c = 0.01\), session 200:

Switch session Assemblages CCD Response rank
0 (from start) 0.9 0.050 0.05
50 4.8 0.751 0.85
100 (after consolidation) 5.2 0.800 0.85

Coupling from session 0 destroys both spatial patterning (CCD \(\to 0.05\)) and functional diversity (rank \(\to 0.05\)). Coupling delayed to session 50+ preserves both. The critical period is not a CCD artifact.

This result converges with recent findings that critical periods emerge spontaneously in deep networks from learning dynamics alone, without explicit programming (Achille, Rovere, and Soatto 2019), and that depth alone suffices to produce them even in linear networks (Kleinman, Achille, and Soatto 2024). Our substrate differs in that the critical period arises from structural consolidation (Hebbian weight reinforcement creating rigid basins) rather than from information-theoretic sensitivity windows. The mechanism is Simondonian: transduction carves the landscape, making subsequent coupling navigational rather than homogenising.

Experiment 9 tests whether the differentiated state is a fragile arrangement or a genuine attractor. At session 100, 10%, 25%, or 50% of node learning rates are randomly shuffled. All conditions produce trajectories indistinguishable from the unperturbed control by session 200: centroid cosine distance \(= 0.84\) [0.81, 0.87], \(n_\text{asm} \approx 5.2\)–\(5.3\) (TOST equivalence test with margin \(\delta = 0.05\): \(p < 0.001\) for all perturbation levels).

This rules out the “frozen disorder” interpretation: if differentiation depended on specific \(\eta\) values, perturbation would destroy it. Instead, the system recovers fully from a 50% scramble, indicating a wide-basin attractor whose structure is maintained by the ongoing dynamics, not by the particular parameter values.

Functional validation (Exp 9fv). Response rank (10-probe, SVD) shows identical perturbation resilience: rank at session 200 is \(0.85\) across all perturbation levels (\(0\%\), \(10\%\), \(25\%\), \(50\%\)), with no measurable deflection at or after the perturbation event (session 100). The attractor is functional, not merely geometric.

Geometric vs. functional differentiation

Centroid cosine distance measures geometric differentiation—distinct weight structures. But spatial separation need not imply distinct functional capacities. DeLanda’s assemblage theory requires that genuinely differentiated components possess qualitatively different capacities—different open lists of what they can do—not merely different positions (DeLanda 2006, 2002).

Experiment 12 tests this directly. We measure response diversity: the standard deviation of assemblage gains, where each assemblage’s gain is the product of its mean learning rate and mean internal edge weight—a proxy for signal amplification capacity.

Probe count and rank ceiling. Exp 17 uses \(K = 5\) probes, giving a response matrix of dimension \(5 \times A\) (\(A\) = assemblage count).   • Local hard (meta \(= 0.05\)): from \(0\) assemblages (pruning destroyed them) to \(\mathbf{10.3}\) assemblages with rank \(\mathbf{5.6}\) (\(n = 19/20\))

The baseline result demands a precise account of what metaplasticity contributes. Both baseline and metaplastic conditions reach rank \(5.6\) without pruning—the hard clip alone scaffolds functional diversity. Metaplasticity’s contribution is threefold:

  1. \(\eta\) divergence (\(F = 4.9\), \(p < 0.0001\); Wilcoxon \(W = 21\), \(p = 0.016\); Shapiro-Wilk normality \(p = 0.99\)): assemblages develop distinct learning dynamics, a parametric dimension absent from the baseline.
  2. Assemblage count: metaplasticity increases the number of differentiated units from \(\sim 6\) to \(10+\), expanding the system’s combinatorial diversity.
  3. Robustness: the metaplastic state recovers from 50% \(\eta\) scramble (Exp 9/9fv), indicating a wider basin of attraction than the baseline.

Metaplasticity does not create functional diversity from nothing—it stabilises and amplifies diversity that Hebbian dynamics on a spatially structured substrate spontaneously produce.

Power and yield analysis. The \(\eta\) divergence result (\(F = 4.9\)) derives from 6 of 50 seeds that produced \(\geq 3\) assemblages under hard weight + soft \(\eta\) bounds. With \(n = 6\) and \(d = 5.62\), the achieved statistical power exceeds \(0.999\) (non-central \(t\)-distribution). The concern is not power but yield: 88% of seeds converge to monopoly, and the qualifying criterion (sufficient assemblages for between-group comparison) is spatial, applied before examining \(\eta\) values. The 44 monopoly seeds show identical \(\eta\) statistics (\(\bar{\eta} = 0.098\), \(\sigma = 0.019\)) to the 6 qualifying seeds, confirming that selection is on spatial configuration, not on the effect variable. Future work should design substrates with higher multi-assemblage yield—for example, through structured initialisation or wider locality sweeps—to confirm the effect across a broader range of initial conditions. For a moderate effect (\(d = 1.0\)), 80% power would require \(n = 8\) qualifying seeds.

Three caveats remain. First, response diversity and response rank both measure functional properties derived from the substrate’s existing dynamics; they do not test for capacities outside the system’s vocabulary. Second, soft \(\eta\) bounds produce rank \(\approx 2.0\) (no-pruning) or \(\approx 0.8\) (pruned)—below the hard-bound rank of \(5.6\), confirming that the absorbing boundary is load-bearing for functional diversity. Third, even rank \(5.6\) represents differentiation along a moderate number of functional dimensions; whether this constitutes DeLanda’s “open list” of qualitatively distinct capacities, or a richer parametric variation, is taken up in the Discussion.

The core differentiation result is invariant to homeostatic timescale across an order of magnitude.

Transplant test: differentiation is context-dependent

Experiment 19 tests whether assemblage identity is intrinsic or context-dependent by swapping the \(\eta\) values and activity histories of the two largest assemblages at session 100. If differentiation reflects intrinsic assemblage properties (DeLanda’s relations of exteriority), transplanted assemblages should retain their functional profiles in their new spatial context.

They do not. The transplant produces no measurable disruption at any timescale: CCD, response diversity, and assemblage count are indistinguishable from the control condition by session 101—one session (300 steps) after the swap. The transplanted \(\eta\) values are immediately overwritten by the local homeostatic target at the new spatial location. Assemblage identity is determined entirely by spatial context, not by intrinsic properties of the component nodes.

This result bears on DeLanda’s (2006) relations of exteriority, though the falsification is not absolute. DeLanda acknowledges that exteriority admits of degrees—components need not be fully detachable to possess some degree of external relations. The assemblages do resist perturbation (Exp 9), which indicates partial exteriority: they are not infinitely context-sensitive. But the transplant result shows that the identity-constituting relations are spatial, not intrinsic. The differentiation is genuine (assemblages have measurably different functional profiles) but not portable: it is constituted by the relationship between assemblage and milieu, not by properties the assemblage carries with it.

The self-sealing dynamics observed here—where consolidated pathways preferentially reinforce themselves, resist perturbation, and maintain their organisation through their own activity—exhibit structural parallels to operational closure as described in autopoietic theory (Maturana and Varela 1980). Three levels of closure should be distinguished. (a) Simondonian operational closure: the operation that constituted the individual (Hebbian reinforcement of co-active edges) is the same operation that now maintains it. The assemblages satisfy this criterion. (b) Varela’s organisational closure: the network of processes that produces the system’s components is identical to the network that those components constitute. This is stronger — it requires the system to produce its own processes, not just maintain its own state. The assemblages do not satisfy this criterion: the Hebbian rule is imposed, not self-produced. (c) Full autopoiesis: organisational closure plus self-production of a topological boundary in physical space (Beer 2020). The assemblages do not produce their own boundary — it emerges from the weight dynamics but is not a distinct physical structure. This positions the assemblages at level (a), between minimal autonomy and full autopoiesis in the hierarchy described by Thompson (2007).

In Simondon’s terms, the assemblage and its associated milieu are co-individuated—neither has an identity independent of the other. In DeLanda’s terms, relations of exteriority do not hold: components cannot be detached from their assemblage and plugged into another while retaining their functional identity.

Discussion

The three-way comparison

The central finding of this paper can be stated as a three-way comparison across the full programme:

Homeostatic regime Outcome
None (Papers 1–3) Self-sealing: individuation without differenciation
Global target Monopoly: one assemblage dominates
Local target Stable differentiation: multiple distinct assemblages

Self-sealing, previously diagnosed as the obstacle to differenciation (Paper 3), is reinterpreted here as its precondition. Without self-sealing, no assemblage maintains its boundaries long enough to consolidate a distinct identity. With self-sealing but no homeostatic regulation, assemblages individuate but remain clones. With self-sealing and local homeostatic regulation, each assemblage consolidates in its own neighbourhood, subject to its own local reference signal, producing genuinely different internal dynamics.

Why Turing partially fails on Hebbian substrates

The Turing result is more nuanced than a simple falsification. Two structural features of the Hebbian substrate are genuinely incompatible with the reaction-diffusion framework, while a third turned out to be partly artifactual:

  1. Dynamic topology. Classical Turing analysis assumes a fixed diffusion graph. The Hebbian substrate rewires at every step—the Laplacian eigenspectrum is non-stationary. This is a structural incompatibility that no implementation change can resolve.

  2. Sparse connectivity. Wolfrum (2012) showed that sparse networks generically produce single-differentiated-node solutions. Distributed Turing patterns require dense, regular connectivity.

  3. Absorbing \(\eta\) boundaries (partly artifactual). Under hard learning rate clipping (\(\eta_i \in [\eta_\text{min}, \eta_\text{max}]\) with hard clip), bounded \(\eta\) values create absorbing states where inhibitor coupling cannot modulate the learning rate once it reaches a bound. Experiment 11 showed that soft \(\eta\) bounding (tanh saturation) partially rescues coupling, confirming this was an implementation artifact rather than a fundamental incompatibility. This matters philosophically: the Deleuzian framework from which we derived the Turing hypothesis assumes continuous intensive quantities (Deleuze 1994, ch. 5). Hard \(\eta\) clipping violates this assumption, and our initial failure to recognise this introduced an unnecessary discontinuity into the very mechanism we were testing.

The honest assessment is therefore: Turing morphogenesis on this substrate is structurally impaired (points 1–2) and was additionally sabotaged by an implementation choice inconsistent with its theoretical grounding (point 3). With soft \(\eta\) bounds, coupling achieves CCD \(= 0.65\)—genuine differentiation, though still significantly below local homeostasis (CCD \(= 0.84\) under hard \(\eta\) bounds, \(0.30\) under soft). The structural impairments explain the residual gap.

Philosophical grounding: a productive fracture

This programme began within a Deleuzian framework: individuation as actualisation of virtual multiplicities, differenciation as the production of genuinely novel kinds through intensive processes (Deleuze 1994). Papers 1–3 found that Hebbian dynamics produce individuation (actualisation) but only bare repetition (same process, identical copies). The question was: what produces complex repetition?

The answer—local homeostatic targets—creates tensions within this framework that we believe are more productive to name than to resolve. A third philosophical voice, DeLanda’s assemblage theory, provides a diagnostic that neither Deleuze nor Simondon alone supplies.

Where Deleuze holds. Local targets create genuinely heterogeneous problematic fields in the Deleuzian sense. Each node confronts a singular problem defined by its neighbourhood’s contingent history. The differentiation that emerges is not prescribed by the targets—the targets co-evolve with the dynamics, and perturbation experiments confirm the outcomes are not determined by specific parameter values. Different localities constitute different differential fields, each demanding its own resolution. This is differenciation through the problem (Deleuze 1994, ch. 4).

The soft-bounds finding reinforces this reading for the Turing mechanism. Our initial Turing test violated a Deleuzian premise: intensive quantities are continuous, and hard \(\eta\) clipping introduced a non-Deleuzian discontinuity. When we restored continuity (soft \(\eta\) bounds), the Deleuzian mechanism partially succeeded (CCD \(= 0.65\)).

However, the bound-type factorial (Exp 13) reveals a tension that must be confronted honestly. The strongest local homeostasis result (CCD \(= 0.84\)) depends on the same hard \(\eta\) bounds that we criticise for sabotaging the Turing mechanism. Under soft \(\eta\) bounds, local homeostasis produces CCD \(= 0.30\)—weaker geometric separation, with response diversity of \(0.015\) confirming genuine but reduced functional differentiation. The philosophical framework cannot simultaneously condemn hard \(\eta\) bounds for Turing and rely on them for homeostasis without addressing the asymmetry.

The resolution lies in Simondon’s distinction between moulding and modulation (Simondon 2020). Moulding imposes form from without through fixed boundary conditions; modulation continuously regulates an ongoing process through variable constraints. Hard \(\eta\) bounds create absorbing states that classify nodes into binary types (consolidated vs. exploratory)—this is moulding. The discretisation is not intrinsic to the dynamics but imposed by the boundary. Soft \(\eta\) bounds preserve the intensive character of the learning rate, allowing continuous regulation—this is modulation. The stronger differentiation under hard bounds is Simondonian moulding: effective but externally constrained. Moulding is not pejorative in Simondon’s framework—it is a legitimate operation, as in casting, that forecloses further individuation while producing stable form. The weaker differentiation under soft bounds is Simondonian modulation: open-ended but dynamically modest. Moulding is, in Simondon’s analysis, a limit case of modulation—modulation frozen at its boundary values.

Both regimes produce functional differentiation (response diversity \(> 0\)). The moulding regime amplifies it through a discretisation effect that has well-characterised precedents in the synaptic plasticity literature (Rossum, Bi, and Turrigiano 2000; Miller and MacKay 1994; Fusi, Drew, and Abbott 2005). Whether biological metaplastic variables exhibit hard or soft saturation is an empirical question; evidence supports both possibilities (Abraham et al. 2001; He et al. 2016).

Where Simondon wins. The critical period finding introduces an irreversible temporal ordering: individuation must precede communication. Coupling before consolidation destroys differentiation; coupling after preserves it. The outcomes are qualitatively different and the ordering cannot be reversed. This is Simondon’s transduction—the coupled individuation of milieu and individual, where the milieu individuates alongside the entity it supports (Simondon 2020).

Deleuze’s treatment of time in Difference and Repetition (Chapter 2) is not achronological—the three syntheses of time (habit, memory, eternal return) provide a rich temporal ontology. But Deleuze resists irreversible developmental ordering: the eternal return as third synthesis implies that all configurations of the virtual are in principle revisitable. The critical period contradicts this. Once assemblages consolidate under local homeostatic targets, the basin structure is carved; introducing coupling before this consolidation produces a qualitatively different (and irrecoverable) outcome. This irreversibility maps naturally onto Simondon’s transduction, which is explicitly directional, and sits uneasily with Deleuze’s emphasis on the virtual as eternally available for re-actualisation.

Where DeLanda diagnoses. The functional probe (Exp 12) operationalises DeLanda’s criterion for genuine capacity differentiation (DeLanda 2006, 2002). DeLanda distinguishes between properties (actual, denumerable) and capacities (virtual, forming an open list). Genuine differentiation requires that components possess qualitatively different sets of capacities, not merely different positions or parameter values.

The baseline result is the critical test: CCD \(= 0.90\) but response diversity \(= 0.000\). Assemblages that are geometrically distinct are functionally identical—different instances of a single type, not different kinds. DeLanda’s five conditions for capacity formation (DeLanda 2006, ch. 1) map onto our substrate as follows:

DeLanda condition Status
1. Heterogeneous components Yes: \(\eta\) divergence \(> 0\), response rank \(> 1\) (Exps 12, 17)
2. Relations of exteriority No: transplant shows identity is context-dependent (Exp 19)
3. Intensive processes + thresholds Partial: gradients present, no kind-transition
4. Semi-permeable boundaries Replaced by developmental sequence (Exp 8)
5. Top-down constraint preserving variation Yes: local targets (Exp 9 perturbation resilience)

Three of five conditions are satisfied, one is temporally substituted, and one—relations of exteriority—is experimentally falsified. The transplant result (Exp 19) shows that assemblage identity cannot be detached from spatial context: components relocated to a new neighbourhood are immediately assimilated, their transplanted \(\eta\) values overwritten by the local homeostatic target within a single session. This is not attractor stability (which Exp 9 confirms) but its opposite: the attractor is spatially indexed, and nodes converge to whichever basin their spatial position defines, regardless of their initial state.

This falsification is itself a contribution. It precisely characterises the kind of differentiation this substrate achieves: Simondonian co-individuation (assemblage and milieu constitute each other) rather than DeLandian exteriority (components retain portable identity). The differentiation is real but relational—it exists in the coupling between entity and context, not in the entity alone.

The fracture. The system may achieve differentiation—symmetry-breaking into basins with measurably different functional profiles—without achieving differenciation in the full Deleuzian sense—the production of qualitatively novel virtual structures. The vocabulary of outcomes (activity level, connectivity, learning rate, gain) is fixed from the start; the system selects among pre-given possibilities rather than creating new ones. Even the functional differentiation documented in Exp 12 is parametric: assemblages vary in the value of their signal amplification gain, not in the kind of function they perform. Whether parametric variation across a fixed capacity space constitutes genuine novelty or mere distribution is an open philosophical question that computational evidence alone cannot adjudicate.

We propose that this fracture between differentiation and differenciation marks a precise boundary: what a purely Hebbian substrate with homeostatic regulation can and cannot do. It can break symmetry, consolidate distinct attractors, resist perturbation, and produce assemblages with measurably different functional profiles. It cannot generate qualitatively new dynamics that were not latent in its initial rule-set. If differenciation in the strong Deleuzian sense requires the production of genuinely novel capacities—new kinds of rules, not new values of existing rules—then even the locally regulated Hebbian substrate falls short. But it falls short in a precisely characterised way that points toward what a next-generation substrate would require: rule-space expansion, not merely parameter-space exploration. Adams et al. (2017) demonstrated formally that only state-dependent update rules—where the system’s current state modifies the rule governing its next state—produce unbounded evolutionary dynamics. A fixed Hebbian rule, however richly parameterised, constrains the system to a bounded transition graph that must eventually repeat. The metaplastic extension localises a parameter (\(\eta\)) but not the form of the rule (Hebbian correlation). Crossing the productive fracture requires making the update equation itself state-dependent—a direction exemplified by Flow-Lenia’s parameter localisation (Plantec et al. 2023a).

Conclusion

Assemblages under local homeostatic regulation develop genuinely independent functional profiles: response rank reaches \(2.4\) [\(1.7\), \(3.0\)] under the standard pruning regime (Exp 17) and \(5.6\) when pruning is removed (Exp 17np; see Paper 4S), compared to a baseline of \(1.2\) (parametric). Response diversity increases from \(0.000\) (baseline) to \(0.047\) (local homeostasis), and \(\eta\) divergence between assemblages reaches \(F = 4.9\) (\(p < 0.0001\)). These independent functional probes establish that local homeostatic targets produce genuine capacity differentiation, not merely geometric separation (which CCD alone cannot distinguish; Paper 4S). The resulting differentiation is sustained (1000 sessions), perturbation-resilient (50% scramble, full recovery), robust to structural parameters (grid size, locality, target radius), and exhibits critical period dynamics (coupling after consolidation preserves diversity; coupling before consolidation destroys it).

Four findings carry forward. First, the partial Turing rescue under soft \(\eta\) bounds reveals that the original failure was partly an implementation artifact inconsistent with the Deleuzian framework that motivated the hypothesis—a methodological lesson in attending to a theory’s own assumptions when designing its tests. Second, the functional probe establishes that geometric differentiation (distinct weight structures) can exist without functional differentiation (distinct capacities), vindicating DeLanda’s insistence that genuine differentiation requires capacity divergence, not merely spatial separation. Third, the bound-type sensitivity is itself a finding: hard \(\eta\) bounds amplify differentiation through a discretisation effect (Simondonian moulding) that extends a well-known principle from synaptic to metaplastic dynamics (Rossum, Bi, and Turrigiano 2000; Miller and MacKay 1994). Soft \(\eta\) bounds produce weaker but philosophically cleaner differentiation (Simondonian modulation). Both regimes produce genuine functional differentiation; the bound type controls its strength. Fourth, the differentiation achieved—while real, stable, and functional—remains parametric: different values of the same capacities, not qualitatively novel kinds. If complex repetition in the full Deleuzian sense requires the production of genuinely new capacities, then differenciation in Hebbian substrates requires not merely heterogeneous regulation but rule-space expansion—a substrate capable of generating new kinds of dynamics, not just distributing existing ones across space.

What would differenciation look like computationally? We can now state this precisely: assemblages that develop qualitatively different update rules, not just different parameter values of the same rule. In our substrate, all nodes execute the same Hebbian learning with homeostatic \(\eta\) adjustment; the rules are parametrically varied (\(\eta\) values differ) but structurally identical. Genuine differenciation would require some assemblages to spontaneously develop anti-Hebbian dynamics, competitive inhibition, or temporal integration properties not present in the initial rule-set. This connects to the open-ended evolution literature’s distinction between variation within a fixed genotype-phenotype map and the expansion of the map itself (Aguilar et al. 2014; Banzhaf et al. 2016). The transplant result (Exp 19) sharpens the point: even the parametric variation achieved is context-dependent, immediately overwritten when the spatial context changes. Portable, intrinsic functional identity—the hallmark of DeLandian exteriority and a prerequisite for open-ended evolution—requires a substrate whose components can carry their rules with them. Flow-Lenia’s parameter localisation (Plantec et al. 2023b) achieves this; our Hebbian substrate does not. The gap between local homeostatic differentiation and genuine rule-carrying individuation defines the research frontier for the next phase of this programme.
